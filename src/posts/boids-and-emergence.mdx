title: Boids and emergent behavior

Each boid (bird-oid) is a simple reactive agent that follows three basic rules: separation, alignment, and cohesion. When you put a bunch of them together, they create lifelike flocking behavior. This demonstrates emergent behavior: complex patterns arising from simple interactions.

The three rules:

1. **Separation**: steer away from boids that are too close
2. **Alignment**: steer toward the average *direction* of nearby boids
3. **Cohesion**: steer toward the average position of nearby boids

No global state or leader, which makes each boid fully autonomous.

The boids algorithm is a classic example of emergent behavior where the flock-level pattern arises from local interactions, not from any top-down instruction. This concept also shows up in neural networks. Individual neurons follow simple activation rules, but together they can learn to recognize complex patterns and make decisions. The emergent intelligence of the network is not explicitly programmed but arises from the interactions of its components.

The Particle Swarm Optimization (PSO) algorithm borrows the same intuition. Particles share their best-known positions with neighbors, and the swarm collectively converges on optima without any single particle needing a full global view of the landscape.
